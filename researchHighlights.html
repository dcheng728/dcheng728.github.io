Infinitely Repeated Prisoners' Dilemma Supergames

The prisoners' dilemma is a classic and powerful example in Game Theory. In the classic setting, the equilibrium of this game would be both players defecting. However, it is known that when the game is repeated, the equilibria can change, making cooperation possible. I was interested in what happens if it were to be repeated infinitely many times.
I abstracted every possible configurations of the game as states, and used finite-state machines to model the dynamics of a infinitely repeated game. I was able to characterize transition properties between states when the utility function satisfies certain constraints: locally non-cooperative and monotone increasing. These properties allowed me to derive asymmetric equilibria in games when the utility function is symmetric! Furthermore, it turns out there is a connection between the asymmetric equilibria that I derived and a famous empirical result obtained by Robert Axelrod in 1981, called "tit for tat". Basically, Robert Axelrod gathered a bunch of game theorists and asked them to implement a strategy using computer programs and then repeatedly play the prisoners' dilemma against each other in a tournament. The strategy that won the tournament was called "tit for tat", which plays cooperate until the other player defects, then plays defect for the rest of the game. Axelrod found this interesting and later showed that the "tit for tat" strategy is an evolutionary-stable equilibrium strategy, a much stronger notion of equilibrium strategy. The purely theoretical results that I obtained in the infinitely repeated prisoners' dilemma turn out to imply the "tit for tat" strategy as the equilibrium strategy in repeated prisoners' dilemma! I later presented this result at the 33rd International Conference on Game Theory, Stony Brook.

Grasp Planning Algorithms

Between October 2020 and June 2021, I looked for ways to reduce the dimensionality for grasp planning algorithms in order to decrease the search complexity. A grasp planning algorithm is one that finds stable ways to grasp an object given the object geometry. The issue with grasp planning is that it searches in a space with high dimensionality. In the past, researchers have reduced the complexity for finger configurations using Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which preserves over 80% of finger variance with only three eigenvectors. In my research, I looked for ways to reduce the complexity of searching for where to place the hand (a.k.a. the hand transformation matrix). I had two theories: (1) the grasp space contain rotational symmetry, (2) there is a many-to-one mapping between object geometry and stable grasps; that is, the same grasp is stable for many different objects. Then I went on to conduct experiments with 60 everyday objects and 28 subjects, to approximate what ratio of the transformation space is needed for producing stable grasps. Turns out, less than 1/4 of the search space can account for over 95% of the 13440 grasps that I recorded, which supports my initial hypothesis that grasp planning can be done in a significantly reduced transformation search space. In addition, my data shows the transformation parameters for human-planned grasps follow normal distribution.

Visualization of a grasp planning algorithm converging using "Graspit".

My work on this project has been organized in an arXiv preprint. The core of this project is the dataset of 13800 grasps from 28 univeristy students. I named this dataset "GUVOOD", that is "Grasps Under Varied Object Orientation Dataset", and it is stored on github.

Arc Pelvis Fixation Path Algorithms

Pelvis fracture typically happen after traumatic injuries such as automobile accidents. This fracture is a serious injury with disability rate of 60% and death rate of 10%. In order to put the fractured pelvis back together, a screw need to fixate the pelvis. Due to the complex and irregular geometry of the human pelvis, a path for a straight screw is not ideal. In the summer of 2021, I worked on designing an algorithm that finds a curved path to put a fixation screw in the pelvis. We took a finite element approach to this problem where we approximated the pelvis' geometric structure to many tiny voxels, then generated constant-curvature paths that would fixate the fractured bone. The finite element approach allows our algorithm choose a tradeoff between efficiency and being realistic by adjusting the voxel size. We later performed computational biomechanics analysis to analyze the constant-curvature paths we generated, and the results indicate a higher safety measure than traditional straight paths.

Soft Robotics

When most people think about robots, they picture a rigid humanoid built with metals. Traditionally, robotics have been built with rigid materials for durability and precision. However, there are tasks that we wish a robotic system can be "soft". For example, when a robot needs to interact with humans or handle delicate objects. At Beihang University between June 2018 and August 2021, I participated in several projects in design and innervation of soft robotics.

Anthropomorphic finger

With another PhD student, we designed a soft robotic finger with three degrees of freedom that can mimick the full range of motion of that of a finger. The finger is 3D printed using nylon with notches carved into it. The notches are arranged in a specific way that it only allows joint-like rotation rather than shrinkage or elongation in the axial direction.

Our finger performing human motions.

Our finger performing in-hand manipulations.

This soft finger proposal has been applied onto both robotic grippers and prosthetic hands. The benefit of using soft fingers on hands is that the soft material is compliant in nature, so it can adapt to the surface geometry of the object it is trying to grasp.

Innervation of Soft Robotics

Soft robotics are superior to rigid robotics in compliance and adaptability, but this comes with the price of much harder kinematic modeling and sensorization. Innervation means "to give something the ability to sense". For traditional rigid robots, the robot's movement in response to actuation is much more predictable: most can be modeled using classical physics or trignometric equations. However, for soft robotics, a lot of times we don't know how it will behave, and this makes innervating soft robotics a persistent challenge in the soft robotics community. We came up with a biomimetic design to allow soft manipulators to sense. The proprioception framework in human body is how we feel where our body parts are at and how heavy things are, when someone is trying to stand on one foot with their eyes closed, they are using their proprioception. We applied this mechanism to soft robotics, and by using machine learning algorithms, we were able to distinguish 8 different textures with a soft robotic finger.

The human proprioception framework (top) and our sensing mechanism (bottom).




Our robotic finger is capable of palpating different objects to identify which is which. It achieves 99% 6-fold cross validation accuracy when identifying 8 different textures.

Price Discrimination Identification in Apple Inc.

Naturally, consumers with different sensitivities towards prices exist in a market. Firms may choose to take advantage of such varying price sensitivity to maximize profit. This practice is called price discrimination or personalized pricing. Apple Inc. is considered by many to have changed the personalized computer and phone industry, it is also once the world’s largest company with market capitalization in the trillions. The iPhone is a popular product from Apple sold across the globe. In fact, the prices of iPhones vary in an interesting fashion across different markets. For example, the iPhone 13 with 128gb of storage is sold at $799 in the U.S. and roughly $1441 in Brazil, a much less developed economy. Selling iPhones in Brazil definitely requires additional costs that may otherwise be avoided in the U.S. But when those additional costs are accounted for, are iPhones still price higher in countries such as Brazil than in the U.S?
I was interested in finding out whether geo-economical factors affect the price of iPhones in different countries when differentiated costs are accounted for. In my work, I adopted Stigler's definition for price discrimination, gathered price of iPhones and socioeconomical data from 53 countries where Apple Inc. sell their products, and performed econometric analysis. I found strong statistical evidence of Apple utilizing its market power to increase profit. Specifically, iPhones are

(1) significantly more expensive (p < 0.01) in markets where Apple has a high market share
(2) significantly more expensive (p < 0.01) in economies with high gini index (high gini index -> high income inequality)
(3) significantly more expensive (p < 0.01) in upper-middle-income economies (another word for developing countries)
